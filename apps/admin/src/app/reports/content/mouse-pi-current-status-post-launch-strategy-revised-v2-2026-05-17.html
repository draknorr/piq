Post-launch strategy — revised draft v2

MOUSE: P.I. For Hire

The visual identity is doing all the commercial heavy lifting. Players who bought on style are recommending conditionally, and every conditional review is a future buyer reading it and passing. The next 90 days are about breaking the caveat.

Verdict

The hook converted. The follow-through hasn't.

Positive reviews and negative reviews name the same product. Buyers praise style, noir setup, jazz score, and combat pace. Then they list four friction clusters — controls, bugs, quest handling, late-game cleanup — and recommend with caveats. Fix those clusters and the recommendation language loses its "but." Sell DLC before fixing them and you confirm the suspicion that you're monetizing an unfinished product.

01 — Highest impact

What should happen next

Players have named the same four friction clusters — controls, bugs, quest handling, late-game cleanup — across reviews, community threads, and creator coverage. The next public beat should answer all four. DLC is a separate, later message.

Each recommendation below names the failure mode, the consequence of sequencing it wrong, and how much of the read rests on observed signal versus inferred sequence.

High confidence Directional signal Strategic inference
01

The next patch has to clear all four friction clusters players have already named.

Across the public Steam review corpus and community threads, negative and mixed language clusters tightly on four areas: input feel and controls, bugs (heavier in the back half of the campaign), post-case progression clarity, and perceived value relative to price. These same four surface inside positive reviews as caveats — the "great style, but..." pattern. The patch needs to answer all four, framed as a response to feedback, not a version changelog.

If you skip one: the conditional-praise pattern doesn't break. Every subsequent sales beat lands inside the same caveat, and the next quarter's conversion rate keeps tracking the launch curve instead of bending.

Highest impact
02

Don't sell more game until the conditional praise breaks.

The Deluxe edition and announced Story DLC are already listed on the store page. Right now, players are actively posting about bugs and controls. Running DLC promotional weight alongside unresolved friction creates a specific perception problem: "they're selling me more game before they finished the first one." Sequence: ship the substantive patch → watch review language shift from conditional to clean over two to three weeks → then activate DLC messaging. The store page can keep DLC visible. Move the promotional weight, not the listing.

If you collapse the beats: the patch message and the DLC message blunt each other. Owners who needed the support proof read DLC promotion as evasion, and wishlisters who needed the DLC pitch hear it through unresolved-friction noise.

03

Lead the store page with the 1930s rubberhose noir hook — before any retro-shooter framing.

Positive advocacy language links four elements together: the rubberhose visual style, the noir detective setup, the jazz score, and the combat pace. Players who recommend MOUSE recommend the gestalt — those four named together far more often than any one of them named alone. The store page currently buries that gestalt under generic FPS framing. Give players who finished the main case an equivalent hook to come back for.

Achievement and progression patterns show a concentrated drop-off around main-case completion. 06 — Timing

Post-launch beat sequence

Three public beats, in order: patch → reactivation → DLC roadmap. The windows below are sized around the competing FPS calendar and the Summer Game Fest attention compression in mid-June. They shouldn't collapse into one message.

Directional signal Strategic inference
May 18-20

Primary patch window

Ship the substantive patch — all four friction clusters answered (controls, bugs, quest handling, post-case loop). The May 18–20 window holds clear air before Summer Game Fest coverage and competing FPS preview cycles compress attention in early-to-mid June.

If you slip past late May: the patch post has to compete with SGF preview coverage and AAA reveals for surface area. The same patch lands with measurably less reach.

Jun 2-4

Backup patch window

Hold this slot only if the patch isn't ready in May. SGF coverage is already ramping by this point; expect noisier signal and weaker discussion. Same message hierarchy — don't let the update read as a changelog.

If the patch isn't substantive: shipping the backup window with thin content burns both the May and June moments. Better to delay one week than to land underweight.

Jun 15-17

Reactivation beat

Give players who finished the main case a specific reason to come back — a tracked case log, a visible collectibles completion board, or a short standalone detective vignette. Post-SGF, the attention market is clearer again. Lead with the detective premise, not with patch notes.

If you skip this beat: the DLC announcement in August lands on a quieter base, and the DLC message has to recruit returning players instead of speaking to a base already reactivated.

Aug 10-11

DLC roadmap window

Spell out what's included in Deluxe, what the Story DLC actually covers, and roughly when. This window sits before gamescom mid-to-late August, so the roadmap surfaces before fall release pressure begins to compete for attention. Tone here is reassurance, not urgency.

If the patch hasn't shifted review language by then: hold the roadmap. Activating DLC messaging while the conditional-praise pattern is still intact converts the trust problem into a backlash problem.

Avoid the most crowded release weeks — competing boomer-style FPS launches and attention-adjacent releases will make a support or DLC message harder to land. Take the earliest patch slot only if the update genuinely includes the player-facing improvements named in the four friction clusters.

07 — Commercial read

What PublisherIQ uncovered

The useful part of this is the sequence, not the diagnosis. Stabilize trust first by answering the four friction clusters. Sharpen the public promise by leading the store page with the four-element identity. Reactivate the base before activating DLC messaging.

Strategic inference

The patch has to clear all four friction clusters or the caveat doesn't break.

Player friction and visible support cadence both point the same direction: the next public beat has to land as a substantive patch answering the named clusters. Partial answers leave the conditional-praise pattern intact, which means every downstream beat — store refresh, reactivation, DLC — lands inside the same caveat.

What happens if you collapse it: the patch reads as maintenance, not as response. Review language doesn't shift, and the support story can't carry the store page.

Move three

Patch, reactivation, and DLC each speak to a different audience.

Owners hearing the patch beat are not the same audience as wishlisters hearing the DLC announce, and neither is the same as completionists deciding whether the post-case loop is worth their time. Collapsed into one message, the beats blunt each other. Separated, each one is easier for its audience to evaluate on its own terms.

What happens if you bundle: the support message reads as DLC pre-promotion, and the DLC message reads as evasion of unresolved friction. Both audiences lose confidence at the same time.

Move four

Sequencing is the deliverable. Support, store proof, timing, and DLC expectation-setting sit on one line.

Other readouts would surface the same diagnosis. PublisherIQ's value is connecting support cadence, store proof, timing context, and DLC expectation-setting into a single execution sequence rather than four parallel conversations. The sequence is what makes the diagnosis actionable.

What happens if you run them in parallel: each track gets touched, none of them land. The four friction clusters stay visible inside positive reviews, and the August DLC roadmap arrives without the trust runway it needs.
